Explore Boston's Neighborhood Inequity

Filter crime incidents from 6/15/2015 - 7/15/2015 on a map and view demographics data for each district

Boston’s 684,379 residents are divided between 23 neighborhoods, as shown on the map to the right.

These neighborhoods are divided off to create Boston’s 12 police districts.

Hover over each police district to find the neighborhoods inside of it.

Note that some neighborhoods cover more than 1 police district.

In our data, we derive the demographics of each police district based on the demographics data of the neighborhoods contained within that district. For neighborhoods that don’t fit perfectly into a single police district., we assign them to the police district that covers the majority of their area.

The Boston Police Department gives crime incident reports detailing the location and type of each incident BPD officers respond to.

In our data, we chose to show only severe crimes, such as larceny (theft), disorderly conduct, and assault.

Shown here is the distribution of severe crimes per police district that occurred from June 15, 2015 to July 15, 2015, binned by each 100.

Certain crimes occur much more often than others.

Shown here is the distribution of the crime types we chose to show in our visualization.

However, this average composition of crime types is not representative of each individual district.

Certain districts are more susceptible to more severe types of crimes than others.

The maps on to the right show the percent that larceny and disorderly conduct make up of each district’s total crimes.

We can see that larceny, unlike disorderly conduct, is more common in northern districts.

To understand more about neighborhoods, we can explore their demographics.

The pie chart on the right shows the race distribution of Boston’s residents.

This data was collected from the 2015 American Community Survey conducted by the US Census.

Similar to how Boston’s crime type composition is not representative of each individual district’s crime types, Boston’s race makeup is not representative of each individual district’s race makeup.

The maps on the right show each district’s percentage of white and black residents, binned by each 10%.

Although Boston has a large minority population, it continues to suffer heavily from segregation due to housing policies from the pre-civil rights era.

In fact, the Boston-Cambridge-Newton metropolitan area ranks 15th in terms of segregation among the 51 large metro areas with significant black populations.
